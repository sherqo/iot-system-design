\subsubsection{Motion Sensor (PIR)}

A Passive Infrared (PIR) sensor detects changes in infrared radiation emitted by warm bodies.
It is compact, low-cost, and energy-efficient.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{9.1.1-pir.jpg}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{9.1.1-grid-eye-illusion.png}
\end{minipage}
\caption{PIR motion sensor (left) and PIR sensing grid illustration (right)}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Sensor details:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Detection range: up to 7 meters
    \item Detection angle: 110 degrees
    \item Operating voltage: DC 4.5V--12V
    \item Output signal: 3.3V digital output
    \item Delay time: 0.3 seconds to 5 minutes (adjustable)
    \item Operating temperature: -15$^\circ$C to +70$^\circ$C
    \item Sensitivity: adjustable
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Working principle summary:}

\textbf{1. Infrared detection:} The PIR sensing element continuously detects infrared
radiation from the environment. When a warm object crosses the field of view, the detected
radiation level changes from one zone to another, producing a measurable differential signal.
This transition is the key trigger used by the sensor to recognize motion.

\textbf{2. Sensor structure:} Uses dual sensing zones and lens focusing.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{9.1.1-infrared-detection.png}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{9.1.1-sensor-structure.png}
\end{minipage}
\caption{Infrared detection concept (left) and PIR sensor structure (right)}
\end{figure}

\textbf{3. Idle state:} Balanced sensing zones keep the output stable.

\textbf{4. Motion detection:} As a person moves across the sensing area, one PIR segment
receives higher infrared energy than the other. The sensor circuit amplifies this difference
and interprets it as movement.

\textbf{5. Output generation:} After threshold comparison, the sensor produces a digital
output pulse. This output is forwarded to the controller to trigger occupancy and automation
logic in real time.

\centering

\caption{Motion detection behavior}
\end{figure}

PIR should be installed away from Wi-Fi antennas to reduce electromagnetic interference risk.

\subsubsection{Air Quality Sensor (IAQ)}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{9.1.2-air-quality-sensor.jpg}
\caption{Indoor air quality sensor module}
\end{figure}

IAQ sensing supports ventilation control by monitoring gases and climate parameters.

\textbf{Key IAQ factors:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Chemical pollutants (CO$_2$, VOCs, NO$_2$)
    \item Biological contaminants (mold, bacteria, allergens)
    \item Particulate matter (PM2.5, PM10)
    \item Environmental conditions (humidity, temperature, pressure)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Common pollutants and impact:}
\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular*}{\textwidth}{@{\extracolsep{\fill}}L{2.2cm} L{4.8cm} L{5.6cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Pollutant} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Health Effects} \\
\midrule
CO$_2$        & Ventilation indicator                  & Fatigue, headache, lower productivity \\
VOCs          & Emitted by paints/cleaners/plastics    & Irritation, dizziness, long-term risk \\
PM2.5/PM10    & Fine dust and smoke particles          & Respiratory and cardiovascular burden \\
CO            & Incomplete combustion product          & Severe toxicity at high concentration \\
Formaldehyde  & Emitted by furniture/insulation        & Carcinogenic, irritation \\
NO$_2$/O$_3$  & Combustion and outdoor sources         & Respiratory distress \\
Humidity      & Moisture level                         & Mold growth, discomfort \\
Temperature   & Thermal condition                      & Fatigue, reduced comfort \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular*}
\caption{IAQ factors and effects}
\end{table}

IAQ sensors require periodic calibration (typically every 6--12 months).

\subsubsection{Water Flow Sensor}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{9.1.3-water-flow-sensor.jpg}
\caption{Water flow sensor}
\end{figure}

Water flow sensors measure instantaneous flow rate and cumulative usage, producing pulse
output suitable for MCU integration. This allows the controller to detect abnormal continuous
flow and apply water-saving actions when needed.

\textbf{Working principle summary:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Water drives an internal rotor.
    \item Hall-effect sensing converts rotor speed to pulse signal.
    \item Pulse frequency maps to flow rate.
    \item Controller computes usage and triggers control logic.
\end{itemize}
